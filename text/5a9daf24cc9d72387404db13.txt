School Audits
(cid:190) Audits are performed in accordance with State
law, which requires OLA to audit all 24 public
school systems once over a six-year period. As
of January 2009, 14 reports have been issued.
(cid:190) The purpose of the audits is to evaluate the
effectiveness and efficiency of the school
system’s financial management practices in
safeguarding assets and its use of financial
resources.
(cid:190) Audit approach consists of 11 functional areas
previously approved by the Joint Audit
Committee, as required by State law.
(cid:190) When warranted, we used the work of other
auditors to avoid duplication of effort.

Overview
(cid:190) FY 07 operating budget of $165 million, including
payroll of $112 million
(cid:190) Enrollment of approximately 14,400 students in
27 schools.
(cid:190) Report contained 21 findings and 13
recommendations in 8 of the 11 areas reviewed.
(cid:190) Revised or additional procedures to improve
efficiency or effectiveness of school system
operations could be implemented in several areas,
particularly transportation.
(cid:190) Control weaknesses were noted in a few areas
including equipment and credit cards.
(cid:190) Certain best practices were found to be in place in
several areas such as procurement and payroll
systems, federal funds, food services and risk
management.

Key Findings – Transportation
Bus Contractors– (p.35-36)
(cid:131) Bus contractors were paid 120 percent of the bus’
cost for depreciation over the life of each bus.
(cid:131) Bus contractors were also paid an annual 9.5%
return on their investment in each bus without
considering market interest rates.
(cid:131) As a result, for the 108 buses purchased between
1997 and 2008, payments to bus contractors will
be $2.6 million higher than necessary.
(cid:131) A requirement that bus contractors be county
residents may violate State and Federal laws.
Electronic Routing Software– (p.35)
(cid:131) WCPS did not use its routing software to assist in
developing more efficient bus routes.
Cost Benefit Analysis – (p.36)
(cid:131) The decision to use a contractor owned bus fleet
instead of school-owned buses has not been
formally reevaluated.

Key Findings – Efficiency and Effectiveness
Human Resources– (p.20)
(cid:131) Work force planning did not include non-
instructional positions.
Facilities and Maintenance – (pgs.31-32)
(cid:131) A documented energy management plan was not
developed.
(cid:131) A formal documented training program for
maintenance employees had not been developed.
(cid:131) The full capabilities of its automated work order
system were not used to control costs and assess
performance.
Food Services– (p.41)
(cid:131) Adopting a higher meals per labor hour goal,
consistent with industry guidelines, could lead to
improved efficiencies in the food service
operations.

Key Findings – Other Issues
Procurement and Disbursements– (pgs.16-17)
(cid:131) Contracts were not always formally approved by
the Board and adequately monitored to ensure
that correct prices were paid.
(cid:131) Credit card disbursements frequently were not
approved by supervisors.
Information Technology – (pgs.26-27)
(cid:131) Certain security measures were not taken to
more effectively control computer access and
user privileges.
(cid:131) An IT disaster recovery plan was not prepared.
Equipment – (p.24)
(cid:131) Procedure are needed for conducting periodic
physical inventories and reporting of lost or stolen
items to improve accountability.
School Board Oversight – (pgs.45-46)
(cid:131) The system’s budget does not contain specific
goals, objectives, and financial-related
performance measures.
(cid:131) A formal ethics policy should be adopted.
