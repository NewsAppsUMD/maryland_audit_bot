School Audits
(cid:190) Audits are performed in accordance with State
law, which requires OLA to audit all 24 public
school systems once over a six-year period.
(cid:190) The purpose of the audits is to evaluate the
effectiveness and efficiency of the school
system’s financial management practices in
safeguarding assets and its use of financial
resources.
(cid:190) Audit approach consists of 11 functional areas
previously approved by the Joint Audit
Committee, as required by State law.
(cid:190) When warranted, we used the work of other
auditors to avoid duplication of effort.

Overview
(cid:190) FY 08 operating expenditures of $48 million,
including payroll of $30 million.
(cid:190) Enrollment of approximately 2,900 students in 10
schools.
(cid:190) Report contained 35 findings and 23
recommendations in all of the 11 areas reviewed.
(cid:190) Internal control problems were noted, such as
accountability over certain cash receipts and
access to the automated procurement and
disbursement and payroll systems.
(cid:190) Policies need to be developed or improved in
certain areas.
(cid:190) Certain operational improvements should be made
to improve effectiveness and efficiency.
(cid:190) Certain best practices were found to be in place in
several areas such as federal grant management,
facilities, transportation and food services.

Key Findings – Internal Controls
Cash Receipts – (p. 11)
(cid:131) Certain collections were not recorded upon
receipt and independent verifications that
collections were deposited were not performed.
Procurements and Disbursements – (p. 19)
(cid:131) Many employees were assigned incompatible
functions such as posting payment
transactions, adding vendors and printing
checks.
Human Resources and Payroll – (p. 24)
(cid:131) One employee had access to all critical payroll
functions such as adding employees, changing
pay rates and preparing checks.
Information Technology – (p. 32)
(cid:131) Passwords features could be used more
effectively, logging and reporting of security
events were not enabled and a comprehensive
disaster recovery plan had not been developed.

Key Findings – Other Issues
Procurement and Disbursements (p. 19)
(cid:131) Procurement policies did not address
contracting for services ($1.7 million in FY
2008) and did not require Board approval of
any non-construction contracts.
(cid:131) Our test of 15 disbursements disclosed that 3 of
these disbursements totaling $208,600 were
not competitively bid.
Facilities Construction and Maintenance– (p. 39)
(cid:131) The capital improvement planning process did
not formally determine the extent and impact of
its deferred maintenance.
(cid:131) Our test of 13 facility-related invoices disclosed
that no bids were received for 4 of these
purchases totaling $52,000.

Key Findings – Other Issues
Transportation – (p. 44)
(cid:131) Rates used to determine payments to bus
contractors were not based on documented
criteria.
(cid:131) Our analysis of one component of contractor pay
disclosed that payments were based on criteria
that may result in unnecessary payments of $1.4
million for buses purchased between 1995 and
2009.
(cid:131) Fuel payment rates made to contractors were
not reduced in accordance with contract terms
to reflect a decline in fuel prices.
Other Financial Controls– (p.60)
(cid:131) SCPS did not verify the authenticity of health
care program participants and dependents.
